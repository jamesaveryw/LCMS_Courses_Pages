Giving customers a great experience can be a challenge. One reason is that the customer decides whether an experience is good—or bad. The customer decides the value of an interaction, and opinions can vary A LOT from customer to customer. Did the customer feel you treated him respectfully? Did he think you were easy to work with? And ultimately, did he think you helped him and met his needs?


Another reason is that companies often have more than one kind of customer. Let's look at the types of customers insurance companies serve.

Who's a Customer?

A life insurance company interacts with many different people in the course of doing business, including

  • A financial professional trying to determine the status of an application
  • A policyowner using his mobile device to change an investment allocation
  • A beneficiary calling to find out how to make a claim
  • The CEO's assistant calling Accounting with a question about an expense report
  • A service representative needing information to answer a customer's question
  • An IT professional emailing HR to find out about her health insurance coverage

The people who own or benefit from our companies' products are obvious customers. In our industry, the financial professionals who distribute our products are also important customers. Even if you never interact with policyowners or financial professionals, your work benefits other people within the company. Those people are your customers! If you provide great service to your customers, that great service will eventually benefit the company's external customers in some way.

Individual and Organizational Customers

Understanding the different needs of individual and organizational customers lets insurance company employees provide service that's specific to each customer's situation. To understand those needs, one must first understand who individual and organizational customers are.

Individual Customers

An individual customer buys or uses a product primarily for his own or his family's benefit. For example, individual customers often buy business insurance products and annuities for their own use and benefit. They buy life insurance on their own lives to provide benefits to other individuals or entities such as

  • Policy beneficiaries or other claimants who receive benefits if the contract owner dies
  • Annuity payees who receive periodic payments from an annuity
  • Employees enrolled in a group life insurance plan offered by their employer

Organizational Customers

An organizational customer—such as a business, government, charity, educational institution, union, or any other organization doing business with an insurer—buys a product or service for its own benefit or the benefit of its employees or members. Click or touch the box below to see an example.

Example

  • Business owners purchasing insurance—including liability insurance, or key person life insurance—to protect the business
  • Organizations purchasing insurance—including life insurance, medical expense insurance, or disability income insurance—for members/employees and their dependents

External and Internal Customers

Click or touch the right arrows below to learn about external and internal customers.

External Customer

An external customer is any person or organization in a position to buy or use a company's products or to advise others to buy or use a company's products. External customers are also known as end customers.

Internal Customer

An internal customer is a company employee or department that receives support from another employee or department within the organization.

Overcoming the Silo Mentality

Without a single, unified vision and cross-functional ownership of the customer experience, employees can easily develop a silo mentality that prevents them from sharing information or knowledge with each other. When this happens, different departments may deliver different customer experiences with different management, different goals, and different ways of measuring success.

For example, if a company's marketing department launches a successful ad campaign without letting the call center know, the customers' satisfaction may decrease. Successful, new campaigns can lead to increased numbers of inquiries. This results in a call center that is understaffed, which can lead to longer wait times and angry customers. If marketing had communicated with the call center, it could have prevented customers from having a bad experience that could reduce the positive impact of the hard work the marketing team put in with the new ad campaign. Without communication and collaboration, everyone loses.

What causes the silo mentality?

Just like the literal silos that separate different types of grain, organizational silos separate different departments. Some factors that lead to the silo mentality include

  • Separation—When different departments have separate physical locations (buildings, floors, rooms, or even different cities), separate mailing lists, or separate goals, the organization becomes fragmented. When a department seems like its own organization, it has become a silo.
  • Competition between teams—While friendly competition can temporarily boost performance and morale, over the long term it may cause employees to develop greater loyalty to their teams than to the company. If people on one team try to stop another team from ''winning,'' someone will certainly lose. If any team in a company ''loses,'' then the company itself loses.
  • Cumbersome legacy systems or incompatible technologies that prevent divisions from sharing information. Source.

Keeping grain separated in different silos is necessary, but in a life insurance company, an employee or division that has a silo mentality most often does not provide good internal customer service. When a company prioritizes cross-departmental communication and collaboration, it encourages internal customers to see each other's viewpoints.

Collaboration between departments allows a company to have a comprehensive view of the external customer, as well. When each department has its own views about what the customer wants and needs, its limited perspective doesn't create a clear view of the customer. When different departments work together to create a 360-degree view of the customer, they overcome the silo mentality and move toward the comprehensive understanding of customers' wants and needs that is required for customer centricity. Collaboration makes for a more pleasant work atmosphere and can lead to the information sharing needed to create a seamless customer experience for both internal and external customers.


Customer-centric insurance companies strive to have seamless processes. When different departments collaborate successfully, internal processes occur seamlessly without inconveniencing the customer. For example, a seamless process might involve a customer changing her address on the life insurance address change part of the company's online customer portal and having the address automatically changed for her annuity and her disability insurance, as well. In contrast, insurers whose processes are not seamless can cause confusion and frustration for a customer if/when communication breakdowns between departments occur.

Financial Professionals as Customers

Financial professionals are some of an insurance company's most important customers because they influence whether or not multiple external customers decide to do business with the insurer. Because financial professionals are a critical link in distributing products and providing service to external customers, most insurance companies try to give financial professionals the highest possible level of customer service.


In fact, some companies consider financial professionals to be the company's primary customers. These companies concentrate on providing service to financial professionals, who in turn are expected to provide service to external customers. These financial professionals may be part of an insurance company's direct distribution system or indirect distribution system, and how the company supports each group varies. Click or touch each heading below to learn more.

Financial Professionals in a Direct Distribution System

In a direct distribution system, the insurer owns the distribution system and communicates directly with the customer through a direct sales force or through direct-to-consumer sales. The company exercises significant control over its direct sales force, which can include career agents, multiple-line exclusive agents, and salaried sales representatives, and also contributes significantly to their support. Home office support for these financial professionals involves recruiting, licensing, training, sales support, compensation, and market conduct monitoring, as well as building and maintaining the technical infrastructure that makes these support systems possible.

    Financial Professionals in an Indirect Distribution System

    In an indirect distribution system, or intermediary distribution system, the insurer relies on independent/non-affiliated individuals and organizations—including independent agents, broker-dealers, registered investment advisers, banks, and other insurance companies—to communicate with and offer products and services to external customers. These intermediaries are generally responsible for their own business expenses, including office expenses, training expenses, marketing expenses and security benefits. They are under no obligation to place business with a specific insurer, so providing a great experience for them is a high priority for insurers.

    Insurers know that intermediaries are more likely to place business with insurers that

    • Communicate effectively with them
    • Offer products aligned with their clients' needs
    • Provide high-quality customer service to them and their clients
    • Pay competitive compensation

    The Takeaway

    An insurance company must offer different types of support for the different financial professionals they call their customers if they want them to have an exceptional experience with the company.

    Business Partners as Customers

    Business partners are organizations that help companies administer their products and/or provide service to external customers. For insurance companies, business partners often include

    • Third-party administrators (TPAs)
    • Outsourcing companies
    • Vendors that perform specific functions to help companies serve their customers

    Business partners are external customers because

    • Their actions can influence how other external customers view the insurer
    • They require service and support from the insurer

    They are internal customers because

    • They work closely with the insurer to meet external customer needs

    The insurer must provide effective and professional service and support or the business partner might have difficultly performing its duties or might end the partnership, making it difficult for the insurer to meet its customers' needs.
