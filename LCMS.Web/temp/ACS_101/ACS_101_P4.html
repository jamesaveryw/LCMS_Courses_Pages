Levels of Customer Service

Learning Objective

Distinguish between basic customer service and exceptional customer service, and explain how exceptional customer service can help bridge the service gap.

Think about some of your experiences as a customer. Chances are the memorable ones were either extremely bad or very good. But what made them so? Click or touch each box below to see some characteristics of poor, ordinary, and exceptional levels of service.
Poor

Poor service doesn't meet customers' expectations and leaves them dissatisfied. Examples include

  • Slow response to customer requests
  • Rude, unprofessional, uninformed, or unsympathetic employees
  • Sloppy record keeping
  • Mishandled transactions
  • Failure to follow through on promises
  • Inability of service providers to answer questions
Ordinary

Ordinary service is the basic level of customer service that customers expect.

  • It meets their needs—what is necessary to create satisfactory conditions.
  • It may even meet their wants—what they'd like to get.
  • If they get the service they expect, they're not wildly excited, but if they don't get it, they're dissatisfied.
Exceptional

Exceptional service goes beyond what customers expect. Instead of offering the benefits and service that routinely come with products, exceptional service provides value-added customer service.

    Value-Added Customer Service

    But what qualifies as value-added customer service? Consider the situations below, and then click or touch each box to learn whether they are examples of value-added customer service.

    In the past, patients had to file paper claims with their health insurers. Now health care providers commonly submit claims directly to the insurer.

    Is this value-added customer service?

    Probably not. Direct filing of medical claims by health care providers may have been an added value at first, but now that most insurers offer this benefit, customers expect it as part of basic customer service. When customers expect a particular service from a company, they'll be disappointed when that service isn't available, but they'll take it for granted when it is available. In other words, they won't consider it to be a value-added service.

    Forthright Financial knows that paperwork can bog down financial professionals. To ease the burden, Forthright created an app that allows agents to complete and submit most required forms on their mobile device.

    Is this value-added customer service?

    Yes. Introducing new technology that makes a financial professional's job easier definitely adds value. However, technology doesn't stay new for long. As more companies introduce similar options and financial professionals begin to expect them, insurers will need to find new ways to surpass expectations.

    The importance of value-added customer service cannot be overestimated. The more valuable a company is to its customers, the more likely its customers will continue doing business with the company and that it will achieve its bottom-line business goals.

    Moment of Truth

    Another key to providing exceptional customer service is managing moments of truth, when the customer forms an impression—good or bad—about the company.

    Watch this scenario that illustrates the concept.

    Sound of a 10-digit phone number being dialed.

    Recorded female voice: Thank you for calling Forthright Financial. Unfortunately, we are experiencing unusually high call volume right now. Please hold and a representative will be with you as soon as possible.

    A few seconds of ''hold'' music plays.

    Recorded female voice: Your wait time is...approximately 10 minutes.

    Carlos Torres:

    Oh, for...10 minutes? Really?

    Continue ''hold'' music.

    Recorded female voice: Thank you for holding. Your call is very important to us. Please stay on the line and someone will be with you momentarily.

    Continue ''hold'' music, then cut off.

    Gloria:

    Thank you for calling Forthright Financial. My name is Gloria. I am so sorry you've had to wait so long. We're experiencing unusually high call volume. I know that must have been extremely frustrating. How can I help you?

    Carlos:

    OK, whatever. Let's not waste any more time.

    Gloria:

    Certainly, sir. How can I help you today?

    Carlos:

    I had a question about my policy... (audio fades and becomes unintelligible.)

    Gloria:

    Was there anything else I can help you with today?

    Carlos:

    No. That was all.

    Gloria:

    Well, thank you for calling Forthright Financial, and I apologize again for the wait.

    Carlos:

    OK. Goodbye.

    When was the moment of truth in this call?
    That's correct!

    Sorry. That's incorrect.
